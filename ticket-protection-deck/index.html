Goals & guardrail

Goals & Metrics

A Goal states the outcome — what success looks like; a Metric measures progress toward it. AARRR ("Pirate Metrics" — Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue) maps a customer journey to the right metric at each stage; HEART is the alternative, for iterating on an existing feature's quality and engagement. The Guardrail is a check on the goal, ensuring that there is no negative impact to improving the metric.

Lens: AARRR — Revenue / Acquisition Depth: Thin
Planning assumption

Goal — checkout attach rate

10–20%

Protect Group's own claimed industry attach-rate figure — the only real benchmark currently in the vault. This is a planning assumption, not a measured target: nothing is live yet, so there's no real conversion data to set a target against. Revisit once a vendor is confirmed and real data exists.

Guardrail

Must not happen as a result

Checkout completion rate drops

Checkout completion rate (tickets actually purchased) must not drop as a result of adding the protection option. The add-on must not cost more in abandoned purchases than it earns in commission — the failure mode a pure attach-rate goal alone wouldn't catch.

Who it's for

Empathising with Users

Personas and Empathy Maps put a face on who we're building for — not to invent detail for its own sake, but to force real focus on someone's context and concerns before designing a solution. A Persona captures who the person is; an Empathy Map digs into what they think, feel, say, and do in the moment that matters, surfacing the Pain that blocks them and the Gain that would actually convert them.

Priya

29, marketing exec, London — occasional ticket buyer

ArrivalDiscovers the marketplace via a Santander Edge Explorer benefit tile inside the banking app she already trusts and uses daily.

Think & FeelHer first reaction to the offer isn't relevance or fear — it's mild confusion. Her mental model is "the platform just sorts it out if something goes wrong," from a past cancelled gig Ticketmaster auto-refunded with no effort on her part.

PainConflates "the event is cancelled" (already covered by the Buyer Guarantee) with "I personally can't attend" (the actual risk this covers) — doesn't register it as something distinct or necessary.

GainClear, simple language distinguishing the two would resolve her actual confusion. Trust in the environment isn't the fight here — Santander's own brand already carries that.

Marcus

42, Warrington, father of two — occasional buyer

ArrivalClicked an ePay affiliate banner mid-way through an unrelated XE.com currency exchange — his first purchase on an unfamiliar marketplace, matching the actual initial launch model (affiliate-only traffic).

Think & FeelGeneral low-grade suspicion of the marketplace itself. Any add-on, protection included, reads first as "another way to extract more money from me."

PainZero existing brand trust to lean on. If the framing feels remotely like an upsell trick — a pre-ticked box, unclear pricing, vague wording — he won't just decline, he may abandon the purchase entirely.

GainRadical simplicity and transparency — a one-line statement of what's covered, an obviously fair price, opt-in never opt-out — plus naming the actual underwriter, since he has no brand trust of his own to draw on.

The real problem

Identifying the Problem

Job Stories (Jobs to Be Done) reframe a feature ask as the real underlying need — not "add a checkout toggle" but the actual problem it solves. Once problems are named, the Opportunity quadrant scores each one on Customer Signal (how strong the problem is for users) against Business Alignment (how much solving it advances the goal) — only the top-right corner is safe to build without question.

Job stories

The ask named a fix, not a problem. One level down: Likewize's own T&Cs make every ticket purchase final-sale, non-refundable — buyers have zero recourse if they can't attend. That's the real, validated core need.

Solution Exploration

Once a solution idea exists, break it into its assumptions and find the Riskiest Assumption — the one most essential to success but least understood — then design a cheap experiment to test it before building, rather than assuming it's true.

Ruled out

Vendor economics

A real commercial risk, but already being actively tested through the live negotiation itself.

Ruled out

Priya's confusion

Reads as a solvable copywriting/UX problem, not a fundamental uncertainty.

Riskiest

Marcus's opt-in behaviour

Most essential to success, least understood — zero real evidence either way.

The riskiest assumption "Buyers arriving via cold ePay-affiliate traffic (Marcus-type, no pre-existing relationship with the marketplace) will opt into a checkout protection add-on at a commercially meaningful rate at all, regardless of how well it's messaged."

This test may not run at all — the checkout embed is architecturally similar to the existing Tixstock/Stripe integration, so the dev lift may be low enough that Andy/Anis skip formal testing and ship-and-learn from real production data instead.

HypothesisAt least a meaningful minority of Marcus-type participants opt in when shown a well-messaged, realistic checkout mockup.

Success threshold≥5% click-through opt-in — set below Protect Group's 10–20% industry figure, since that reflects direct-intent primary-ticketing buyers, not cold-affiliate traffic.

The solution — story map

Story Mapping & Minimum Feature Set

Story Mapping breaks the user's journey into themes (the backbone) and the tasks under each, ranked by Cost of Delay — how much value is lost by not shipping something sooner. The Minimum Feature Set line marks the first release: the smallest genuinely useful slice, not a cut-down or buggy one.

User, goal, context: a buyer, mid-checkout, having already decided to buy a specific ticket — wants confidence they won't lose their money if something stops them attending. Peace of mind, not "add protection," is the actual goal.

MFS line

Theme 5 stays in scope — a product with no real way to claim isn't delivering real value — but it's near-zero build effort since it's entirely vendor-hosted. Cut below the line: example coverage scenarios and extended cover-details retrieval — leaner v1, add later if signal supports it.

True dependency: vendor selection blocks Themes 2, 3 and 5 — copy, price, underwriter name, and claims path can't be finalized until a vendor is confirmed.
